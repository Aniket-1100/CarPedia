SUPRA MK1

The Toyota Supra MK1 is the first generation of the iconic Toyota Supra, The Toyota Supra MK1 was also known as the A50 model. marked the beginning of the Supra lineage. Produced from 1978 to 1981, the MK1 Supra shared its platform with the Toyota Celica but was positioned as a distinct, more performance-oriented model. The MK1 Supra featured a distinctive and sportier design compared to the standard Celica. It had a longer nose, unique headlights, and a more pronounced front grille. The styling aimed to convey a sense of performance and sophistication.

SUPRA MK2

The Toyota Supra MK2 is the second generation of the iconic Toyota Supra, The Toyota Supra MK2 was also known as the A60 model. Produced from 1981 to 1986, the MK2 Supra built upon the foundation laid by its predecessor, the MK1, and set the stage for the more widely recognized MK3 and MK4 Supra models. The MK2 Supra featured a more modern and aerodynamic design compared to its predecessor. It had pop-up headlights, a long hood, and a distinctive rear end. The design aimed to provide a balance between sportiness and luxury. While the MK2 Supra may not have achieved the same iconic status as its successors, it remains a classic among collectors and enthusiasts of vintage Japanese sports cars.

SUPRA MK3

The Toyota Supra MK3 is the third generation of the iconic Toyota Supra, The Toyota Supra MK3 was also known as the A70 model. It was produced from 1986 to 1992 and represented a significant evolution from its predecessor, the MK2. The MK3 Supra was known for its sleek design, improved performance, and technological advancements. The MK3 Supra featured a more streamlined and aerodynamic design compared to its boxy predecessor. It had pop-up headlights, a long nose, and a distinctive rear spoiler. The design reflected the automotive trends of the late 1980s.

SUPRA MK4

The Toyota Supra MK4 is the fourth generation of the iconic Toyota Supra, which made its debut in the market back in 1993. The Supra MK4 was built on the Lexus SC platform, but it was 13 inches shorter than the Lexus. Powering this beast was a 3.0L sequential turbo straight-six engine that churned out an impressive 320 hp of maximum power. The Supra MK4 gained immense popularity among enthusiasts due to its blend of performance, style, and reliability. It became a cult classic and is still highly sought after by collectors and fans of Japanese sports cars. Unfortunately, production of the fourth-generation Supra ended in 2002.

Dodge Challenger First Genration

Introduced in the fall of 1969 for the 1970 model year, the first-generation Dodge Challenger was one of two Chrysler E-body cars (the other being the Plymouth Barracuda). Positioned to compete against the Mercury Cougar and Pontiac Firebird, it was a response to the Ford Mustang. Built on the Chrysler E platform, it came in hardtop and convertible body styles, sharing major components with the Plymouth Barracuda. Engine options included the iconic 426 Hemi V8 and the 440 Magnum V8. The Challenger R/T and T/A models are highly collectible today.

Dodge Challenger Second Genration

The second generation of the Dodge Challenger spans the model years 1978 to 1983. This era marked a departure from the first-generation Challenger, which was produced from 1970 to 1974. The second-generation Challenger was smaller and more compact, reflecting the changes in the automotive landscape during that time. The design of the second-generation Challenger was influenced by the styling trends of the late 1970s and early 1980s. It featured a more squared-off and aerodynamic shape compared to the first-generation model. The car was available in various body styles, including a coupe and a three-door liftback. The second-generation Challenger offered a range of engine options, including four-cylinder and V6 engines.

Dodge Challenger Third Genration

The Dodge Challenger third generation encompasses the models produced from 2008 onwards. The third-generation Challenger marked a significant revival of the iconic muscle car, blending modern technology with the classic design cues that made the Challenger a legend in the 1970s. It embraced a retro-inspired design, incorporating elements such as the split grille, round headlights, and a prominent hood scoop reminiscent of the original model.The lineup included a range of V6 and V8 engines, with some of the higher-performance trims featuring HEMI V8 engines, delivering substantial horsepower and torque.

Nissan Skyline-1st Gen

The Nissan GT-R, often referred to as the "Godzilla," has a rich history that spans several decades. The first GT-R badge appeared on the Nissan Skyline in 1969 with the introduction of the PGC10 Skyline GT-R. This was a high-performance version designed for racing, featuring a 2.0-liter inline-six engine. It became known for its success in motorsports. Notably, it won 50 consecutive races in the 1969-1971 seasons, contributing to its nickname "Hakosuka," a combination of "hako" (box) and "suka" (short for "Skyline").
