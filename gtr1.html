Nissan Skyline -1st Generation

❮ ❯

The first Skyline GT-R, known internally as PGC10, made its debut on February 4, 1969. Initially exclusive to Japanese Nissan dealerships under the Nissan Prince Store, this high-performance car was born from the merger of Nissan and Prince Motors in 1966. The early Skyline GT-Rs were produced between 1969 and 1972, carrying the model code KPGC10. These cars quickly gained fame by dominating Japanese touring car racing events. Although their production run was short-lived, they continued the legacy of performance excellence. The Nissan Skyline GT-R, often referred to as the "Hakosuka" (which translates to "boxy Skyline" in Japanese).

1. Engine Options:

It featured a 2.0-liter inline-six engine known as the S20 engine. The S20 engine was a street version of the prototype R380 racing engine. Top Speed: Around 200 km/h. The engine was equipped with three carburetors for fuel mixture control. Its head was hand-ported and polished for optimal performance. Power Output: Approximately 160 horsepower at 7000 rpm.

2. Trim Levels:

During its first generation, the Nissan Skyline GT-R was available in a single trim level. It was marketed as a sporty version of the sedan, emphasizing its racing heritage. These GT-Rs were successful in Japanese touring car racing events. Despite its sedan appearance, it was a high-performance sports car.

3. Customization Options:

During the first generation of the Nissan Skyline GT-R, customization options were more limited compared to modern standards. However, enthusiasts found ways to personalize their GT-Rs. Owners could enhance engine performance by upgrading components such as intake systems, exhausts, and turbochargers. Suspension tuning was crucial for better handling. Adjustable shocks, stiffer springs, and sway bars were popular upgrades. Brake upgrades improved stopping power, especially for track use.

4. Production:

Limited Production: Production of the first-generation GT-R was limited, with only around 1,945 units produced. Discontinuation: Production ceased in 1972, largely due to changing emissions regulations and economic factors.

5. Rarity and Collectibility:

Due to its limited production numbers and age, original examples of the first-generation GT-R are increasingly rare and sought after by collectors. Restorations: Due to its historical significance, there is a growing interest in restoring and preserving first-generation GT-Rs to their original condition. Modifications: Many enthusiasts modify these cars for increased performance or personalized aesthetics, with aftermarket parts readily available.

6. Design:

Exterior: The design was characterized by its boxy shape, which earned it the nickname "Hakosuka." It featured a distinctive front grille and quad headlights. Interior: The interior was relatively basic, with a focus on driver engagement rather than luxury features. Dimensions: The first-generation GT-R had a compact footprint, typical of cars from that era.

7. Global Recognition:

Export Markets: While primarily marketed in Japan, some examples of the first-generation GT-R found their way into export markets, particularly Australia and New Zealand, where they gained a following among motorsport enthusiasts. Legacy in Motorsport: The GT-R's success in motorsport helped establish Nissan as a formidable competitor in racing circuits around the world, contributing to the brand's overall reputation for performance and innovation.

8. Racing Heritage:

Success in Touring Car Racing: The GT-R's racing achievements weren't limited to Japan; it also gained recognition internationally, particularly for its victories in touring car racing events. Bathurst 1000: Notably, the first-generation GT-R made an impact at the Bathurst 1000 endurance race in Australia, showcasing its performance capabilities on a global stage.

9. Limited Production:

With only around 1,945 units produced during its production run from 1969 to 1972, the first-generation GT-R remains a rare and highly sought-after collector's car, adding to its allure and exclusivity, leading to increased appreciation for its historical significance and contributions to automotive engineering and design.

10. Distinctive Design:

The Hakosuka's boxy shape and quad-headlight design became instantly recognizable, contributing to its iconic status in automotive culture. The design was not only aesthetically appealing but also functional, providing improved aerodynamics and stability at high speeds. Powered by the legendary S20 inline-six engine, developed from the Prince GR8 engine used in the R380 racing car. The S20 engine featured advanced technology for its time, including dual overhead camshafts and hemispherical combustion chambers.
